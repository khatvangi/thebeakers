DNA Replication

Molecular Biology

What is it?

DNA replication is the process by which a cell copies its DNA accurately before cell division. It involves unwinding the double helix, synthesizing new strands, and ensuring fidelity through specialized enzymes. This process is essential for genetic inheritance and cellular function.

Key Concepts

Helicase unwinds the DNA double helix to separate the strands.

DNA polymerase adds complementary nucleotides to each strand, following the base-pairing rules.

The leading strand is synthesized continuously, while the lagging strand is made in fragments called Okazaki fragments.

Okazaki fragments are later joined by ligase to form a continuous strand.

The process is semi-conservative, meaning each new DNA molecule contains one original and one newly synthesized strand.

Think of it Like...

Imagine DNA replication as building two identical ladder copies from one. Helicase acts like a zipper to untangle the ladder, polymerase adds rungs to each side, but one side is built in short segments (Okazaki fragments) that are later connected by ligase.

Example

In bacteria, during replication, helicase separates the DNA strands. Polymerase builds the leading strand smoothly, but the lagging strand is made in pieces. These pieces (Okazaki fragments) are stitched together by ligase, ensuring both strands are complete and complementary.

Watch Out For

Thinking both DNA strands are synthesized continuously (forgetting Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand).

Confusing the roles of helicase and polymerase (helicase unwinds, polymerase builds).

Believing ligase is only involved in joining Okazaki fragments (it also seals nicks in the DNA backbone).

Connections

Links to DNA structure (double helix) and the role of base-pairing rules.

Relates to the central dogma of molecular biology (DNA → RNA → protein).

Connects to genetic mutations if replication errors occur.
