Limits & Continuity

Calculus

What is it?

Limits and continuity form the foundation of calculus, enabling us to analyze how functions behave near specific points and ensure they don't have abrupt changes. Continuity ensures functions are predictable, while limits provide the tools to rigorously define derivatives and integrals.

Key Concepts

A limit describes the value a function approaches as the input nears a specific point.

A function is continuous at a point if its limit exists, the function is defined there, and the limit equals the function's value.

Epsilon-delta definition formalizes limits by quantifying 'closeness' using inequalities.

L’Hôpital’s Rule simplifies indeterminate forms (like 0/0) by taking derivatives of numerator and denominator.

The squeeze theorem bounds a function between two others to determine its limit.

Think of it Like...

Imagine a drone flying toward a target. The limit is the target's location, while continuity ensures the drone reaches it without sudden jumps or crashes. Epsilon-delta is like setting a safety zone around the target, and L’Hôpital’s Rule helps adjust the drone's path if it gets stuck in a loop.

Example

Consider f(x) = x². As x approaches 2, f(x) approaches 4. Using the epsilon-delta definition, for any tiny ε > 0, we can find a δ such that |x - 2| < δ implies |x² - 4| < ε. The squeeze theorem also applies to sin(x)/x as x→0, where |sin(x)/x| ≤ 1 bounds the limit to 1.

Watch Out For

Confusing continuity with the existence of a limit (e.g., thinking a function is continuous if the limit exists, ignoring the function's value at the point).

Misapplying L’Hôpital’s Rule to non-indeterminate forms like 5/2.

Mixing up epsilon and delta in the formal definition, such as letting delta depend on x instead of being a universal value.

Connections

Limits are essential for defining derivatives and integrals.

Continuity is a prerequisite for differentiability and the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Epsilon-delta formalism underpins rigorous analysis in advanced mathematics.
