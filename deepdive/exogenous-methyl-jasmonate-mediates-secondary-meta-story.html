Exogenous Methyl Jasmonate Mediates Secondary Metabolic Reprogramming to Enhance Resistance in Tea Plants

The Challenge

Tea plants are an important crop for many countries, but they face significant challenges from insect pests. These pests can cause substantial yield and quality losses, making it difficult for tea farmers to maintain a sustainable livelihood. One of the most common pests affecting tea plants is the green leafhopper, which can transmit diseases and reduce yields.

The problem is not just economic, but also environmental. Insecticides are often used to control pest populations, but these chemicals can harm beneficial insects and contaminate soil and water. So, there's a need for a more sustainable solution that can help tea plants resist pests without harming the environment.

Methyl jasmonate (MeJA) is a potential solution. It's a signaling molecule in plants that can trigger defense responses against insect attacks. But its effects on tea plant growth and quality are not well understood, making it difficult to use effectively.

The Approach

To investigate the effects of MeJA on tea plants, researchers conducted field experiments using Camellia sinensis 'Zhongcha 108' as the test plant. They applied different concentrations of MeJA (0.02–20 mM) to the plants and measured its growth traits, quality components, and resistance to insect pests. The researchers also used transcriptome analysis to understand the underlying molecular mechanisms.

The experiment was designed to find an optimal concentration of MeJA that balances pest resistance induction with growth modulation and processing suitability for flat-type green tea. This required careful planning and execution to ensure accurate results.

Scene 3 of 5

The Innovation

The results showed that MeJA concentrations between 0.2–2 mM had the most significant effects on tea plant growth and resistance to insect pests. These treatments optimized bud morphology, reducing internodes, thickening stems, and altering leaf insertion angles. Importantly, these changes did not significantly affect the quality-related biochemical components of the tea leaves within the evaluated time frame.

This study provides new insights into the integrated effects of MeJA on tea plant growth, resistance, and quality-related traits, offering practical guidance for tea plantation management.

Scene 4 of 5

The Results

The study demonstrates that MeJA can be used as a tool for tea plant resistance, without compromising growth or quality. The optimal concentration window of 0.2–2 mM provides a practical guideline for tea farmers and researchers to apply MeJA effectively in their management strategies.

This research has important implications for sustainable agriculture practices, offering an alternative to synthetic pesticides that can harm the environment. It also highlights the potential of natural signaling molecules like MeJA in promoting plant defense mechanisms.

Why It Matters

The study's findings have significant implications for the tea industry and sustainable agriculture practices. By understanding how MeJA affects tea plant growth, resistance, and quality, researchers can develop more effective management strategies that balance pest control with environmental sustainability.

This research also contributes to our knowledge of plant signaling molecules and their potential applications in agriculture. As we face increasing challenges from climate change and pest management, innovative solutions like MeJA are crucial for ensuring food security and promoting sustainable livelihoods.
