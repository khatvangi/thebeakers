**Measuring Agricultural Economic Resilience in Indonesia: A Comprehensive Analysis**

**ONE-SENTENCE CLAIM**: The Agricultural Economic Resilience Index is a novel measure that assesses the economic resilience of Indonesian provinces by integrating multiple indicators, providing policymakers and stakeholders with valuable insights to inform agricultural development strategies.

**WHY IT MATTERS**: Understanding agricultural economic resilience is crucial for Indonesia's food security and sustainable development goals. With rapid population growth and urbanization, ensuring the long-term viability of agriculture in Indonesia requires a comprehensive understanding of its economic underpinnings. This study provides a data-driven approach to measure AER, which can inform policy decisions and support evidence-based agricultural planning.

**THE MECHANISM**: To construct the AER Index, six core indicators were selected: Agricultural Gross Regional Domestic Product (GRDP), Active Agricultural Labor Force, Per Capita Protein and Energy Consumption, Dietary Desirable Pattern (DDP) score, and Paddy Production Volume. The independent variables used in the subsequent econometric analysis include key determinants such as Investment, Kredit Usaha Rakyat (KUR) credit access, Paddy Insurance uptake, Fertilizer Subsidies, Agricultural Machinery Aids, Irrigation Infrastructure, Agricultural Wage Levels, and Environmental Quality Index (IKLH). The data is analyzed using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for the AER Index Variables and Fixed General Linear Regression (FGLS) for the determinant variables. By integrating these indicators, the study aims to capture the complex relationships between economic factors and agricultural resilience in Indonesian provinces.

**CURRICULUM BRIDGE**: This study connects to undergraduate concepts in economics, statistics, and environmental science. Undergraduate students studying econometrics will recognize the use of FGLS regression analysis, while those exploring environmental issues will appreciate the consideration of environmental quality indicators. Additionally, this study contributes to the understanding of agricultural development strategies, aligning with courses on agricultural economics, food security, and sustainable development.

**WHY TO DOUBT IT**: One limitation of this study is its reliance on a limited number of provinces in Indonesia (34 out of 476). Future research should aim to expand the dataset to more provinces, allowing for broader generalizations and more robust conclusions about AER Index validity across different regions.

**TAKEAWAY**: The Agricultural Economic Resilience Index provides a valuable tool for policymakers and stakeholders to assess agricultural development strategies in Indonesia. By understanding the complex relationships between economic factors and agricultural resilience, this study contributes to evidence-based decision-making and supports long-term food security and sustainable development goals in the country.
