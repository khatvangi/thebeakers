Summary

**Title:** Understanding Self-Efficacy Beliefs in Interdisciplinary Science Teaching: A New Instrument and Its Validity

**Abstract:** This article discusses the development of a new instrument, the Self-Efficacy Beliefs of Interdisciplinary Science Teaching (SElf-ST), which measures teachers' confidence in their ability to teach science effectively. The study examines the validity of this instrument using data from 590 pre-service, trainee, and in-service biology, chemistry, and physics teachers.

**ONE-SENTENCE CLAIM:** The Self-Efficacy Beliefs of Interdisciplinary Science Teaching (SElf-ST) instrument has been shown to have strong convergent validity with self-efficacy beliefs in primary education and divergent validity with other related constructs.

**WHY IT MATTERS:** Understanding teachers' self-efficacy beliefs is crucial for improving science education outcomes. By developing a reliable measure of these beliefs, educators can better support teacher development and provide targeted interventions to address weaknesses. This study contributes to the growing body of research on self-efficacy in science teaching, shedding light on a rarely explored aspect of construct validity.

**THE MECHANISM:**

A. **Conceptualization**: Self-efficacy is defined as an individual's confidence in their ability to succeed in specific situations or tasks. B. **Development**: The SElf-ST instrument was developed based on a pedagogical content knowledge model, which emphasizes the importance of teachers' subject matter expertise and pedagogical skills in shaping student learning outcomes. C. **Validation**: Data from 590 pre-service, trainee, and in-service biology, chemistry, and physics teachers were analyzed to examine the validity of the SElf-ST instrument.

**CURRICULUM BRIDGE:** This study connects to undergrad concepts in science education by highlighting the importance of self-efficacy beliefs in teacher development. Students of education or science education may want to consider how this research informs their own teaching practices and the role of self-efficacy in shaping student learning outcomes.

**WHY TO DOUBT IT:** One limitation of this study is that it relies on a cross-sectional design, which may not capture the longitudinal effects of the SElf-ST instrument. Further research could use longitudinal designs or intervention studies to more fully explore the validity and effectiveness of the SElf-ST instrument.

**TAKEAWAY:** The Self-Efficacy Beliefs of Interdisciplinary Science Teaching (SElf-ST) instrument has been shown to be a reliable measure of teachers' confidence in their ability to teach science effectively. This finding can inform teacher development programs and support targeted interventions to improve science education outcomes.
