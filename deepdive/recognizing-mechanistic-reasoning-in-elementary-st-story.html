Recognizing mechanistic reasoning in elementary students' engineering design discourse

The Challenge

In engineering design, students need to think carefully about how their ideas will work together to solve a problem. Engineers face many challenges, but one of the biggest is figuring out what needs to be done to make something work. For example, imagine designing a playground for kids with disabilities. How can you make it accessible and safe for everyone? This is just one of the many problems that elementary students face when designing engineering prototypes.

The Approach

To tackle this challenge, researchers used existing frameworks from science education literature to analyze data from classroom engineering activities. They looked at how third- and fourth-grade students discussed their designs in progress during two different curriculum units: one on retaining walls and the other on accessible playground structures.

Scene 3 of 5

The Innovation

By connecting these frameworks to new data, researchers discovered that elementary students' mechanistic reasoning discourse in engineering involved identifying the target performance of a design and naming entities involved. This means that students were thinking about how their designs would work together to achieve a specific goal.

Scene 4 of 5

The Results

Across these different design challenge contexts, researchers found that students' mechanistic reasoning discourse was consistent in some ways, but also reflected the unique challenges of each design problem. This suggests that students are developing a general understanding of how to think about engineering designs, even if they're working on very different projects.

flowchart LR G[Consistency] G --> H[Differences] H --> I[General Understanding]
Scene 5 of 5

Why It Matters

Understanding how elementary students think about engineering designs can help educators and curriculum developers create more effective learning experiences. By recognizing the kinds of mechanistic reasoning that students use, we can support their development of critical thinking skills and prepare them for success in engineering careers.
