Impact of structural and material parameters on sensitivity of engineered N-pocket DGTFET biosensors

The Challenge

Biosensors are crucial for monitoring various aspects of our lives, such as health, agriculture, and environment. However, traditional FET-based biosensors have limitations like high subthreshold slope, leakage current, and poor detection of neutral biomolecules. This can lead to inaccurate results and unreliable detection methods.

To overcome these challenges, researchers are exploring alternative technologies that can provide higher sensitivity, label-free detection, and improved performance.

Scene 2 of 5

The Approach

Researchers have been working on developing new materials and designs for FET-based biosensors. One promising approach is the use of Tunnel Field-Effect Transistors (TFETs), which utilize a band-to-band tunnelling mechanism to provide steep switching characteristics and low-power operation.

However, TFETs have their own set of challenges, such as low ON-current and ambipolar conduction issues. To overcome these limitations, researchers proposed a new design for a double-gate TFET featuring an N-pocket and AlGaAs-based heterostructure.

Scene 3 of 5

The Innovation

The proposed N-pocket DGTFET features a GaSb–AlGaAs–GaAs heterostructures, dual-gate control, bilayer dielectrics, and optimised doping profiles. This design enhances tunnelling efficiency and sensitivity compared to traditional silicon-based and non-pocket devices.

The researchers used Silvaco ATLAS simulations to analyze the device's performance and identify key parameters that affect its sensitivity.

flowchart LR A[Traditional silicon-based devices] --> B[N-pocket DGTFET: GaSb–AlGaAs–GaAs heterostructures] C[Bilayer dielectrics and optimised doping profiles]
Scene 4 of 5

The Results

The results show that the proposed N-pocket DGTFET achieves a subthreshold swing of 9.2 mV/dec, an I on /I off ratio of 4 × 10^13, and a reduced threshold voltage of 0.32 V. These values surpass those of traditional silicon-based and non-pocket devices.

The sensitivity analysis indicates that the device exhibits notable improvements with rising dielectric constant, molar fraction, and positive biomolecule conditions. However, negative biomolecules diminish sensitivity due to repulsive interactions.

Scene 5 of 5

Why It Matters

The proposed N-pocket DGTFET demonstrates a significant enhancement in selectivity, achieving sensitivity values of up to 1.20 × 10^5. This exceeds the performance of previously reported TFET biosensors by multiple orders of magnitude.

The findings suggest that the modified DGTFET serves as a reliable, energy-efficient, and highly sensitive platform for label-free biomolecule detection. This technology has far-reaching implications for various fields, including healthcare, agriculture, and environmental monitoring.
