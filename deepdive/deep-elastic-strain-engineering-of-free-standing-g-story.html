The Challenge

Gallium nitride (GaN) is a key material used in blue light-emitting diodes and laser diodes due to its direct band gap and wide band gap. However, this alloying approach has limitations: it can't dynamically modulate the band gap or emission color. Researchers need to find a way to control and change the optical properties of GaN without these limitations.

The Approach

To overcome the limitations, researchers developed a new method to introduce ultralarge tensile elastic strain into microfabricated single crystal GaN microbridges. This approach allows for dynamic and reversible modulation of the band gap and optoelectronic properties. The strain is applied using a special process that manipulates the material's structure.

Scene 3 of 5

The Innovation

By introducing ultralarge tensile elastic strain, researchers can modulate the band structure and optoelectronic properties of GaN. This leads to a significant reduction in the band gap energy, causing the emission color to shift from invisible or UV to the blue visible regime. The results are promising, with substantial band gap reductions observed.

flowchart LR A[Start] | --> B[Strain-Induced Change] | --> C[Blue Visible Emission]
Scene 4 of 5

The Results

Experimental results show that the CL emission of deeply strained GaN microbridges has a substantial band gap reduction, ranging from ∼3.4 to 2.96 eV. This corresponds to an optical emission shift from invisible or UV to the blue visible regime. The calculation of the change in band gap with increased strain agrees well with the experimental results.

Scene 5 of 5

Why It Matters

This research opens up new possibilities for device applications in power electronics and optoelectronics. The dynamic, reversible, and well-controllable strain engineering allows for novel devices that can modulate their optical properties. This could lead to breakthroughs in fields like displays, lighting, and more.
