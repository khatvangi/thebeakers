Two bHLH Transcription Factor Genes AhWSC1a and AhWSC1b Act as Gatekeepers of Testa Pigmentation, Preventing White Seed Coats in Peanuts

The Challenge: White Seeds

In agriculture, seed coat color plays a crucial role in determining nutritional quality and market appeal. In peanuts, white seeds are considered undesirable due to their lower nutrient content and reduced market value. The development of high-quality peanut varieties with desirable seed coat colors is essential for improving crop yields and farmer incomes. However, the genetic control of seed coat color is complex and not fully understood.

The Approach: Uncovering the Gatekeepers

To tackle this challenge, researchers used a QTL-seq approach to identify genetic variants associated with seed coat color. They analyzed a recombinant inbred line population derived from black-testa and white-testa parents. By studying these genetic variations, they discovered two bHLH transcription factor genes, AhWSC1a and AhWSC1b, that play a critical role in regulating seed coat pigmentation.

Scene 3 of 5

The Innovation: Role of AhWSC1

The researchers found that AhWSC1 acts as a rate-limiting hub in the anthocyanin biosynthesis pathway, regulating proanthocyanin production. They demonstrated that AhWSC1 physically interacts with the R2R3-MYB regulator AhTc1 to form a functional MBW complex that activates late anthocyanin-pathway genes. This discovery highlights the importance of AhWSC1 in controlling seed coat pigmentation.

flowchart LR A[AhWSC1] --> B[MBW Complex] C[Activate Late Anthocyanin Genes] D[Proanthocyanin Production]
Scene 4 of 5

The Results: Breaking Down the Barrier

The study revealed that recessive loss-of-function mutations in AhWSC1a and AhWSC1b abolish proanthocyanin biosynthesis, resulting in white testa. The researchers also found that the black testa phenotype may arise from elevated AhTc1 expression associated with a structural variant. However, introducing AhWSC1 mutant lines suppressed AhTc1 expression, indicating that AhWSC1 is an indispensable gatekeeper of seed coat pigmentation.

Scene 5 of 5

Why It Matters: Future Implications

This research has significant implications for peanut breeding and seed production. Understanding the genetic control of seed coat color can help breeders develop high-quality peanut varieties with desirable traits. The discovery of AhWSC1 as a key regulator of anthocyanin biosynthesis also sheds light on the complex interactions between transcription factors and gene regulation in plants.
