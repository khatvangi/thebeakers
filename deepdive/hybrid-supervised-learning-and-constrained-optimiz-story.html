The Challenge

Tendon-driven continuum robots (TDCRs) are robots that can bend and stretch like human tendons. They have many advantages, such as being able to manipulate objects in complex environments. However, solving the problem of telling the robot where to move its end-effector (the part that interacts with the environment) is very difficult. This is because the robot's movement is not linear, but curved, and there are uncertainties in how much force the tendons can apply. Current methods for solving this problem often don't work well enough, so researchers need to find a better way.

The Approach

Researchers came up with a new method that combines two types of learning: supervised deep learning and constrained optimization. The first part, supervised deep learning, uses artificial neural networks to make an initial guess about where the end-effector should be. This is like using a rough map to get started. Then, the second part, constrained optimization, refines this guess by applying physical constraints to ensure that the solution is not only good but also physically possible. This is like adding more details to the map to make it accurate.

The Innovation

What's new about this approach is that it combines the strengths of both supervised deep learning and constrained optimization in a way that has never been done before. This allows for fast initial guesses and precise refinement, making it possible to achieve high accuracy even with complex environments. The researchers also generated a large dataset of optimal solutions using constraint optimization, which they used to train their neural network. This dataset covers different types of trajectories and end-effector orientations, so the method can be applied to various tasks.

The Results

The researchers tested their new method on a two-segment tendon-driven continuum robot and found that it outperformed standalone neural or optimization-based approaches. The new method achieved micrometer-level positional accuracy and micro-degree-level orientation precision, which is much better than previous methods. This means that the new method can be used for real-time applications, such as robotic manipulation in complex environments.

Why It Matters

This new method has important implications for future robotic research and applications. With the ability to precisely track task-space trajectories, tendon-driven continuum robots can be used in a wide range of tasks, such as assembly, inspection, and manipulation of complex objects. Additionally, this method can inspire new design approaches for soft robotics and other types of robots that need to interact with their environment in complex ways.
